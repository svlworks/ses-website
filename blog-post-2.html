Navigating the "Jock Tax": What Every Entertainer Needs to Know

By The Sports & Entertainment Society | June 2026

For most professionals, paying income tax is a straightforward affair: you pay taxes in the state where you live and work. But for professional athletes, touring musicians, and performing artists, the tax code is vastly more complicated, thanks to a system of state income tax rules affectionately (and sometimes bitterly) known as the "Jock Tax."

What is the Jock Tax?

The Jock Tax is the colloquial term for the income tax levied by states and cities against visiting professionals who earn income within their borders. Because athletes and entertainers travel across the country to perform, they technically earn a portion of their annual income in every single state they visit.

If an NBA player plays a game at Madison Square Garden, the state of New York and New York City want their cut of the player's salary for that day of work. Multiply this across an 82-game season, spanning dozens of states with different tax rates and filing requirements, and the administrative burden becomes monumental.

The Duty Days Calculation

States typically calculate the Jock Tax using the "Duty Days" method. This involves taking the total number of days an athlete spent working in a particular state (games, practices, mandatory team meetings) and dividing it by the total number of days in the season. That percentage is then applied to the athlete's total salary to determine the taxable income for that state.

Not Just for Athletes

Despite the name, the Jock Tax applies to almost any highly compensated individual traveling for work. Touring musicians, their crew members, traveling doctors, and even high-profile corporate executives are subject to these same laws. For a major recording artist on a 50-city tour, failing to file non-resident tax returns in every state can result in massive fines and audits.

Why Generalist CPAs Fail Here

A standard CPA is simply not equipped to handle a multi-state tax return of this magnitude. They are unlikely to know which states offer reciprocity agreements, how signing bonuses are apportioned (or shielded) from state taxes, or how to properly deduct un-reimbursed agent fees under current tax laws.
